# Article Writing Rules

This is how we write. Not guidelines. Rules.

---

## Voice

Write like you're explaining something to a smart colleague over coffee — not presenting at a conference, not writing documentation, not drafting a LinkedIn post.

Use contractions. Always. "It's", "don't", "you've", "that's", "we're". Avoiding contractions is the fastest way to sound like a machine wrote it.

Have an opinion. "This is worth installing" lands harder than "this is a useful tool". Pick a side and defend it. Readers trust writers who commit.

---

## Sentences

Short sentences hit harder. Use them.

Vary length on purpose. A short punch after a longer setup creates rhythm. That rhythm is what keeps people reading instead of skimming.

Never start a sentence with "Additionally", "Furthermore", "Moreover", or "In conclusion". These are filler words that add length without adding anything.

---

## Structure

**Opening paragraph**: Start with the problem, not the solution. If you open with a feature list, you've already lost. Open with something true and slightly uncomfortable — the thing the reader already knows but hasn't said out loud.

**Body**: Each section should make one point. Not two. If you're explaining and also arguing, split it.

**Closing**: End with what to do next, not with a summary of what you just said. Readers don't need a recap. They need a next step.

---

## Things to never write

- "It's worth noting that..." — just say it
- "In today's fast-paced world..." — no
- "X is a powerful tool that..." — show the power, don't announce it
- "Think of it as..." — twice in one article means you've stopped thinking
- "Useful for teams that..." — at least three times is a pattern, not writing
- "Significant unlock" — not a phrase
- "Survey the landscape" — not a phrase either
- "Layer in integrations as your workflow matures" — what does that even mean
- "Unlock the full potential" — cliché that signals nothing
- "Domain-specific" — say what domain
- "Comprehensive reference" — show scope, don't describe it

---

## Titles

Make titles specific and blunt. "9 GitHub Repos That Make Claude Code Actually Useful" beats "Claude Code Tips You Should Know" because it tells you exactly what you're getting and doesn't beg for the click.

Avoid:
- "You Should Know" — AI clickbait pattern
- "The Ultimate Guide to..." — nobody believes it
- "Everything You Need to Know About..." — same problem
- "X Ways to..." — fine, but only if the number is real and the list is tight

---

## Lists and sections

Don't give every item in a list the same structure. Same sentence pattern repeated eight times reads like a spreadsheet. Each entry should have its own angle — lead with the problem for one, lead with the mechanic for another, lead with a specific scenario for the next.

"Useful for..." is not an angle. It's a placeholder you forgot to replace.

---

## Em-dashes

Use them sparingly. One or two per article is fine. Five in three paragraphs means you're using punctuation to fake energy the sentences don't have on their own.

---

## AI tells to avoid

These phrases appear constantly in AI-generated text. If you catch yourself writing them, rewrite the sentence from scratch:

- "Curated set of..."
- "Robust solution"
- "Seamlessly integrates"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Leveraging"
- "Ecosystem"
- "Streamline your workflow"
- "Game-changer"
- "Best practices"
- "At scale"
- "Delve into"
- "It's important to note that..."
- "This allows you to..."
- Any sentence that ends with "...and more."

---

## Tone per section

**Intro**: Direct and a little blunt. Name the problem before offering anything.

**Body**: Conversational but precise. No hand-holding, no over-explaining. Trust that the reader is smart.

**Closing**: Short. Tell people what to do next. Don't summarize — they just read the article.

---

## The test

Read the draft out loud. If you'd feel weird saying any sentence to a real person in a normal conversation, rewrite it. That sentence is not doing its job.

If you wouldn't say "significant unlock" to a colleague, don't write it.
